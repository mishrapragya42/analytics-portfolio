Where did our users drop?

A B2C retail checkout funnel was quietly bleeding revenue across multiple steps. Leadership flagged it. I found where, why, and what to do about it — and the A/B test proved us right.

01 · Context

The brief

Leadership noticed purchase conversion had been declining for two consecutive quarters — and brought it to the product org. No one knew exactly where in the journey users were dropping. The funnel was broken somewhere between "browse" and "buy," but the dashboards at the time only showed the top-line number.

It landed on my plate with a fairly open brief: "Conversion is down. Tell us where and why." No pre-existing hypothesis, no scoped dataset. Just a declining metric and an expectation to figure it out.

Dataset: 90 days of event-level clickstream data from a B2C retail platform (anonymised). ~4.5M events across 1.4M users. Supplemented with the public Multi-Category Store dataset (Kaggle) to validate patterns.

My role

End-to-end — I scoped the analysis, pulled the data (SQL on Redshift), cleaned and explored it (Python/Pandas), built the funnel and segmentation cuts, designed the experiment with the PM, and presented results to leadership. There was no analyst handing me a clean dataset. The messy parts were mine too.

Timeline

Initial analysis: ~6 days (data was messier than expected). Experiment design + alignment: 2 weeks. Results readout: 5 weeks post-launch.

Stakeholders

Product Manager, Growth Lead, Engineering Lead, VP Product.

02 · Methodology

How I approached it

🔍

1. Data Audit

First thing I did — before writing a single funnel query — was audit the event data. Found missing cart events for ~8% of sessions and inconsistent session boundaries across device types. Fixed those first. A funnel built on bad event data gives you confident wrong answers.

🏗️

2. Funnel Definition

Spent half a day with the PM just aligning on definitions — what counts as "checkout started"? Does a product view require a minimum dwell time? We used a strict ordered funnel with a 24-hour session window. It gave more conservative numbers, but everyone trusted them.

🔬

3. Segmentation First

The overall funnel looked "manageable" — about 2.7% end-to-end conversion. But averages hide everything. Once I cut by device, user tenure, and traffic source, the picture changed completely. The problem wasn't spread evenly. It was concentrated.

Key call I made: I resisted pressure to share early numbers at day 2. The data wasn't clean yet and I knew a preliminary readout would anchor the team on wrong figures. I shared a "here's what I'm finding and when I'll have something solid" update instead — and it was the right call.
03 · The Funnel

Where users dropped

Out of 1,400,000 users who visited the homepage over 90 days, only 38,200 completed a purchase — an overall conversion rate of 2.73%. The funnel made it immediately clear this wasn't a single problem.

Reading the funnel

This wasn't a single broken step — drop-off was spread across the journey. But two points stood out as fixable, not just expected:

Homepage → Product View (57% drop)
Large, but partially expected — most traffic is browse. The concern here was that mobile drop was significantly worse (63%) than desktop (49%). A gap that size doesn't happen by accident.
Cart → Checkout (52% drop) ← Where I focused
This one was anomalous. These users had already picked a product. Half of them weren't making it to checkout. That's not a discovery problem — that's a friction problem.
Checkout → Purchase (18% drop)
Acceptable. Once users reached checkout, most completed. This told me the payment flow wasn't the issue — it was everything before it.
04 · Deep Dive

Drilling into cart abandonment

With cart → checkout as the primary drop-off, I ran three cuts of analysis to understand who was abandoning and under what conditions.

By Device
By Traffic Source
By User Cohort
Cart → Checkout conversion by device

Finding

Mobile users convert from cart to checkout at 34% — versus 61% on desktop. That's a 27 percentage point gap. Given mobile accounts for 58% of all sessions, this is the single largest addressable lever.

Session recordings (sampled) showed mobile users frequently abandoning when asked to create an account before checkout. Desktop users had a "guest checkout" shortcut more prominently surfaced.

Hypothesis: Friction in the mobile checkout entry (mandatory sign-up prompt) is causing disproportionate mobile abandonment.
Cart → Checkout conversion by traffic source

Finding

Paid social traffic (Instagram, Facebook) has the lowest cart-to-checkout rate at 28%, despite having reasonable add-to-cart rates. Organic and direct traffic converts at 2x that rate.

This suggests paid social audiences are more impulse-driven — they add to cart but lose intent or get distracted. The fix here is speed: reducing time between cart and payment.

Hypothesis: Paid social users need a faster path — consider a "buy now" shortcut on product pages for retargeted users.
Cart → Checkout by user tenure

Finding

New users (first session) abandon at 71% at the cart stage. Users who have purchased before abandon at only 31%. The gap narrows significantly after just one prior purchase — suggesting trust and familiarity are the barriers, not product fit.

Hypothesis: New users need trust signals at the cart stage — reviews, security badges, easy return policy — to overcome first-purchase hesitation.
05 · Experiment Design

The A/B test we ran

Based on the mobile + new user findings, I worked with the PM and engineering to design a targeted experiment. We prioritised the mobile guest-checkout hypothesis as highest confidence and highest reach.

Parameter Detail
HypothesisSurfacing guest checkout more prominently on mobile will increase cart-to-checkout conversion for new users
ControlExisting mobile cart page — "Sign in to checkout" CTA prominent, guest option in small text below
Variant"Continue as Guest" made equal prominence to sign-in; sign-in moved to a post-purchase prompt instead
Primary metricCart → Checkout conversion rate (mobile, new users)
Guardrail metricsAccount creation rate (floor: 60% of baseline), Revenue per session
AudienceNew mobile users · 50/50 split · ~85,000 users/week in segment
Power calculationMDE: 5%, α=0.05, power=0.8 → minimum 2 weeks runtime
Pre-agreed stopping ruleCall result at week 4 regardless — written into the experiment brief before launch
Result✓ Significant · called at week 4
The uncomfortable middle: By week 3, the variant was clearly trending positive — but we weren't at the pre-agreed runtime yet. Stakeholders started asking daily if we could call it early. I held the line. Not because I was being rigid, but because we'd agreed on week 4 for a reason — and changing the rules mid-experiment because the result looks good is how you get false confidence. We called it at week 4, significance held, and nobody questioned the result.

06 · Results

What moved

+22%

Cart → Checkout (mobile, new users)

Primary metric. Statistically significant (p=0.003). The variant outperformed control for 3 consecutive weeks before we called it.

+14%

Overall purchase conversion

The mobile segment is large enough that a 22% improvement in one step translated to a 14% lift in the top-level conversion metric.

−3%

Account creation rate

Within guardrail (−3% vs −40% threshold). Post-purchase sign-up prompt recovered ~67% of what was lost at cart entry.

Revenue impact (estimated): Based on average order value and monthly purchase volumes, a sustained 14% conversion lift translates to approximately ₹1.8Cr in incremental annual GMV for this segment — without any additional acquisition spend.

08 · Reflection

What I'd do differently

What worked well

Segmenting before forming hypotheses. I didn't go in with a theory — I let the data suggest where to look. The overall funnel seemed "okay" until you cut by device, and then it was obvious. If I'd started with a hunch, I might have looked in the wrong place entirely.

Setting guardrail metrics and a stopping rule before the experiment launched. When stakeholders started asking to call it early at week 3, I could point to what we'd all agreed on. It's much easier to hold a pre-agreed boundary than to argue one in the moment when pressure is high.

What I'd do differently

The stopping rule was written into the brief — but it wasn't explicit enough about what "calling it early" would require. Stakeholders kept asking for results before we had significance, and while I held the line, it created unnecessary friction every week. Next time I'd add a clear clause: "Early call requires p < 0.01 AND 3 consecutive weeks of consistent direction." That way the bar for an exception is spelled out upfront, not negotiated under pressure.

I'd also loop in engineering a week earlier in the hypothesis phase. We lost time because the variant required a UI change that turned out to be more complex than assumed — something an engineer in the room at day one would have flagged immediately.
